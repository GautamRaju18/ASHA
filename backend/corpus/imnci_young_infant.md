# IMNCI — Young Infant Age Up to 2 Months (incl. Newborn)

> Starter corpus, paraphrased from public IMNCI young-infant content.

### CHUNK
source: IMNCI Chart Booklet (Young Infant up to 2 months)
section: Possible serious bacterial infection / very severe disease — danger signs
condition: neonatal sepsis
age_group: neonate
urgency_tag: emergency
is_danger_sign: true
---
In a young infant aged up to 2 months, check for signs of POSSIBLE SERIOUS
BACTERIAL INFECTION or very severe disease. Danger signs include: not feeding
well or unable to feed; convulsions; fast breathing (60 breaths per minute or
more); severe chest indrawing; movement only when stimulated or no movement at
all; temperature 37.5°C or above (fever) OR below 35.5°C (low body temperature /
hypothermia); and grunting. ANY of these signs in a young infant is an EMERGENCY.
Keep the infant warm, support feeding/breastfeeding, give pre-referral treatment
within scope, and refer URGENTLY to hospital.

### CHUNK
source: IMNCI Chart Booklet (Young Infant up to 2 months)
section: Local bacterial infection / jaundice
condition: newborn infection
age_group: neonate
urgency_tag: urgent
is_danger_sign: true
---
A young infant with a red or draining umbilicus, skin pustules, or yellow palms
and soles (deep jaundice), or jaundice appearing on the first day of life or
lasting beyond 14 days, needs same-day assessment at a health facility. Jaundice
of the palms and soles, or any danger sign, is consistent with severe illness and
requires urgent referral.

### CHUNK
source: IMNCI Chart Booklet (Young Infant up to 2 months)
section: Low birth weight and feeding problems
condition: feeding problem
age_group: neonate
urgency_tag: urgent
is_danger_sign: false
---
Assess every young infant for feeding. A low-birth-weight infant, an infant not
well attached to the breast, feeding fewer than 8 times in 24 hours, or one
receiving other foods or drinks needs feeding support and close follow-up. If the
infant is not feeding at all, that is a danger sign requiring urgent referral.
